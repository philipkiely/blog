Applied Personal Finance for Independent Creators

| First Posted 13 June 2019 | Last Edited 13 June 2019 | Philip Kiely |

Many people on the internet want to tell you what to do with your money. Spend. Save. Invest. These articles often offer conflicting advice, but that doesn’t mean that most of them are wrong. Rather, as with any system, a financial system must be built with a goal, and different goals will result in very different systems. The goal of my system is building a stable, robust financial engine that supports independence in my life and work.

I should offer two disclaimers at the onset. First off, this will be a long article. Second, this is close enough to the sort of advice that people get paid for giving that I should say that I’m not a lawyer, accountant, financial advisor, or in any other way officially licensed to talk about this stuff. Nothing in this article constitutes investment advice, tax advice, or legal advice. Just because this works for me does not mean that it will be right for you, I encourage you to modify the system to meet your lifestyle and goals.

A sprinkle of early-retirement saving, corporate finance, personal finance best practices, and the following assumptions:

  • You have a small business, creative practice, or consulting operation that you make or intend to make your living from.
  • You are making enough money from that or other sources to pay your living expenses, or you have enough savings to cover the period that you intend to go without income while building something.
  • Certain taxation and investment assumptions are based only on my experience as an American, and may not be the same for all Americans and will almost certainly be different for citizens of other countries.

My system makes it easy to answer the questions:

  • Can I afford to buy this car/computer/vacation?
  • If I stopped making income today, how long before I run out of money without changing my lifestyle? What lifestyle changes would extend that period the most?
  • In the long term, am I increasing my net worth and saving for retirement?

IMAGE: Chart of Accounts

A personal finance system has two components: accounts (where the money lives) and an accounting system (how you keep track of the money). There are four categories, or levels, of accounts in personal finance: Cash, Operations, Funds, and Wealth. The accounting system can run on anything, from a spreadsheet to dedicated software like Quickbooks. Different levels have different tracking standards, and transers between levels are always recorded in the accounting system.

IMAGE: Cash inputs and outputs

Cash is money that is not tracked (except for transfers to and from other levels). Cash can be spent on anything, it is the grease that keeps everything running smoothly. Cash is self-limiting, if I am spending enough of it to make it worth tracking, then I am getting it from somewhere, and that somewhere must be an account under Operations, so it is tracked. This keeps the system reasonable to operate and reduces time spent tracking unimportant spending. Note that Cash does have to only mean paper money, for example, a gift card that you get as a credit card reward could go in this category.

IMAGE: Operations inputs, internals, and outputs

Operations are the foundational accounts that regulate regular earning and spending. For example, a single checking account that keeps a couple months of expenses and is the main entry point for money (for example, direct deposit from jobs and payout from companies) combined with a high-yield savings account with six to twelve months of living and lifestyle expenses comprise the ideal Operations category. The savings account is also how I make payments. Ideally, income is regularly exceeding expenses, so the savings account fills up and serves as the emergency fund.

IMAGE: Funds inputs and outputs

Funds are long-term savings accounts for a particular goal. A single high-yield savings account is an excellent vehicle to begin the Funds category. Funds are for funding things, from new investments and projects to big-ticket purchases. It makes “can I afford to do X” an easy question to answer: if there is enough money in Funds you can, if not, you can’t.

IMAGE: Wealth inputs, internals, and outputs

Wealth is the final and most important category. While building wealth, money never leaves accounts in this category except in true emergencies. Policies around wealth can be revisited once these accounts generate nontrivial investment income or are of sufficient size for purchases like real estate. Until then, only add to and invest from Wealth accounts.

How does Business factor into this?

Taxes, especially around funds and operations. Small businesses basically only have funds and operations: all spending should be tracked, so there is no Cash, and any money that would be saved as Wealth in a perosnal system is paid as a dividend to you, where it can support your living, increase your net worth, or fund unrelated projects.
